preface: "A possible definition of Greekness. During the Persian invasion of Greece in 480 BCE, the Persians asked the Athenians to make a separate peace. Sparta was concerned that Athens would agree, so they sent ambassadors to shore up Athens’ resolve. The Athenians mention shared Greek identity as a reason why they would never side with the Persians against the other Greeks, but this definition was highly controversial and should not be taken as a universally agreed-upon definition of Greekness."
footnotes:
  - Xerxes, the king of Persia.

The Athenians answered the Spartan ambassadors as follows: “It is only human that the Spartans are very afraid that we would come to an agreement with the barbarians. But it is also shameful to be afraid, since you know that the Athenian attitude is that nowhere on earth is there so much gold or land of such exceptional quality and beauty that by receiving it we would be willing to side with the Persians to enslave Greece. [2] There are many important considerations that prevent us from doing this, even if we wanted to. First and most important are the temples and statues of the gods that have been burned and demolished, for which we are forced to get vengeance to the highest degree, rather than come to an agreement with the man who did these deeds.<sup><a href="#note-1">1</a></sup> And then there is Greekness – the fact that we share the same blood and the same language, and the shared temples and sacrifices to the gods, and similar customs – which would not be proper for the Athenians to betray. [3] Know this now, if you did not know it before, that as long as even one Athenian survives, we will never make an agreement with Xerxes.”
