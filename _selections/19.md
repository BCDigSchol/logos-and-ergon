Before the Trojan War, the Greeks apparently did no shared deeds together. [2] It seems to me, in fact, that the Greeks did not even have the name “Hellenes” at all, but that this name did not exist before the time of Hellen, the son of Deucalion.<a href="#note-1">1</a></sup> Instead, the various districts were named after the various tribes, especially the Pelasgians. When Hellen and his sons became powerful in Phthiotis and were invited to help various other cities, each group came to be called Hellenes through associating with them, though this name did not prevail for a long time. [3] The best evidence of this is Homer: although the Trojan War was much later, nowhere does he name the whole army as Hellenes, or any part of it except the followers of Achilles from Phthiotis, who were the first Hellenes. Instead, he calls them Danaans and Argives and Achaeans. Nor does he use the word “barbarians,” since the Greeks had not yet been separated off with a single counterbalancing name.
